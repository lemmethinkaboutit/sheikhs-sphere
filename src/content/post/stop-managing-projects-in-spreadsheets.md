There's a specific moment I've watched happen in a dozen organizations. Someone opens "the tracker" — the master spreadsheet that runs a launch, a portfolio, a hiring pipeline — and three tabs in, they go quiet. Then: "Wait, which version is this?"

That silence is the sound of a process that has outgrown its tool. And almost nobody catches it in time, because the spreadsheet never *announces* that it's failing. It just gets slowly, expensively worse while everyone keeps using it because it's right there and it's free.

I want to talk about that moment honestly — because I've been the person staring at the broken tracker, and I've been the person brought in to replace it after it cost someone a deadline.

## The spreadsheet was never the problem

Let me say the unpopular thing first: spreadsheets are genuinely brilliant, and most "spreadsheets are killing your business" takes are nonsense written by people selling the replacement.

A spreadsheet is the fastest way on earth to model a problem you don't fully understand yet. When you're figuring out *how* a process should work, nothing beats a grid you can bend in any direction. I still start almost every new process in one. If you're tracking eight projects with three people, a sheet is not just fine — it's the correct choice. Don't let anyone shame you into buying software you don't need.

The problem isn't the spreadsheet. The problem is that a spreadsheet has no opinion about how it's used, and that neutrality is exactly what turns toxic at scale.

## How to actually tell it's time

Forget the vendor checklists. Here are the real signals, the ones I look for when someone asks whether they've outgrown their sheet. You don't need all of them. Two or three is usually enough.

**1. Nobody trusts the numbers without asking a human.** The truest test. If the answer to "are we on track?" is "let me check with Sarah" rather than "let me open the sheet," the sheet has stopped being a source of truth. It's become a thing that *represents* the truth that lives in Sarah's head. When Sarah is on leave, you have no process — you have a hostage situation.

**2. Updating it is someone's actual job.** Watch for the person who spends Friday afternoon "cleaning up the tracker." That's not admin overhead — that's the cost of the tool, showing up as a salary. A process should serve the people in it, not employ one of them as its janitor.

**3. The questions you can't answer are the important ones.** "Show me everything blocked by legal." "Which projects slipped twice." "What's the total value of work waiting on one person." A sheet answers what's *in a column*. The moment leadership starts asking questions that span rows, tabs, and time, you're fighting the tool instead of using it.

**4. Two people made changes at the same time.** And now there are two versions, both called "final," and an argument about which is real. Version control is the single thing spreadsheets are worst at and the single thing growing operations need most.

**5. The same status update gets typed three times.** Into the sheet, into the email, into the standup deck. Re-keying the same fact in three places isn't a workflow — it's a tax you pay in attention, and it's where errors breed.

If you read those nodding, the sheet has quietly become the bottleneck. The good news is that fixing it is far less dramatic than the software vendors want you to believe.

## What to do instead — without blowing up your team

Here's the part most "migrate to a platform!" advice skips: the danger isn't staying on spreadsheets too long. It's jumping to a heavy platform too fast, badly, and creating a system *worse* than the sheet you left — because now the chaos has a login screen and a six-figure invoice.

I learned this the expensive way, which is the same thing I wrote about in [why change management is mostly therapy](/posts/change-management-90-percent-therapy/): the tooling is the easy 10%. The 90% is the humans. 
So move in this order.

![Diagram: the four-step path from spreadsheet to workflow, and a side-by-side of what each costs you](/spreadsheet-to-workflow.svg)

**Step 1 — Map the process the sheet is actually running.** Not the one in the SOP document. The real one. Who creates an item, who has to approve it, what makes it "done," where it gets stuck. The spreadsheet is hiding a workflow inside its columns — find it before you automate it. Automating a broken process just lets you be wrong faster.

**Step 2 — Separate the data from the workflow.** A sheet smashes these together. Your replacement should keep them apart: the *records* (the projects, the requests) in one place, and the *flow* (who does what next) as something the system drives. This single separation is what kills the "type it three times" problem — the status lives once and shows up everywhere.

**Step 3 — Move intake first, reporting last.** The highest-pain point is almost always the front door: how work comes in. A simple intake form that routes and timestamps automatically removes more chaos than any dashboard. Build the form, let the data accumulate cleanly for a few weeks, *then* build the executive view on top of clean data. Teams that build the dashboard first end up with a beautiful chart of garbage.

**Step 4 — Keep one foot in the old world during the switch.** Run the new system alongside the sheet for one full cycle. Yes, it's double work for a few weeks. It's also the difference between a migration people trust and one they quietly route around. People don't abandon the spreadsheet because you turned off the spreadsheet. They abandon it when the new thing has earned it.

As for *what* you move to — that genuinely matters less than people think, and far less than getting the four steps above right. A well-configured shared database (Airtable, Notion, a proper project tool) handles most cases. A workflow platform earns its keep once you have real approval chains, governance, or audit requirements. The tool is a detail. The process design is the whole game.

## The honest caveats

A few things the migration won't fix, so you're not surprised:

- **A new tool won't fix an undecided process.** If nobody can agree what "done" means, the platform will just surface that disagreement faster and more publicly. Decide first.
- **You will lose the spreadsheet's flexibility, and it'll sting.** The grid let anyone do anything. The new system has opinions, and someone will be annoyed they can't just type into a cell. That friction is the *point* — but warn people, or they'll resent it.
- **Migrations have a valley.** Weeks two and three feel worse than the spreadsheet did. Everyone's learning, nothing's habit yet. The teams that succeed are the ones told in advance that the dip is normal and temporary.

## The actual cost of waiting

Here's what I want you to take away. The spreadsheet doesn't bill you. There's no invoice, so it feels free — and that's exactly why it's dangerous. Its real cost shows up as the launch that slipped because two people had different versions, the great hire who burned out doing manual updates, the deal that stalled because nobody could see it was stuck. Invisible, until it isn't.

You don't need to panic-buy software. You need to notice the moment the tool stopped serving the work and quietly started taxing it — and then move deliberately, front door first, humans in mind.
